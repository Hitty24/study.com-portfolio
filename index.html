Full Blood Count Explained: Understanding Your Blood Test Results

Have you ever had blood drawn and wondered what all those numbers on your lab report actually mean? A full blood count (FBC) – also called a complete blood count (CBC) – is one of the most common blood tests doctors order. Here's what it measures and why it matters.

What Is a Full Blood Count?

A full blood count is a simple blood test that measures three main types of cells in your blood:

  1. Red blood cells (RBCs) – carry oxygen from your lungs to the rest of your body
  2. White blood cells (WBCs) – fight infection and disease
  3. Platelets – help your blood clot to stop bleeding

The test also measures hemoglobin (the protein in red blood cells that carries oxygen) and hematocrit (the percentage of your blood made up of red blood cells).

Red Blood Cells: Oxygen Delivery

Your red blood cells contain hemoglobin, which binds to oxygen. When doctors look at your RBC results, they check:

MeasurementWhat It Means
Low RBC countPossible anemia, iron deficiency, or blood loss
High RBC countPossible dehydration, lung disease, or living at high altitude

Normal ranges for adults: Men: 4.7–6.1 million cells per microliter | Women: 4.2–5.4 million cells per microliter

White Blood Cells: Your Immune System

White blood cells are your body's defense team. A high WBC count often means your body is fighting an infection. A very low WBC count can put you at risk for infections.
Normal WBC range: 4,500–11,000 cells per microliter

Platelets: Clotting Control

When you get a cut, platelets rush to the site and form a plug to stop bleeding. Low platelets can cause easy bruising or bleeding problems.
Normal platelet range: 150,000–450,000 platelets per microliter

Important Note

A single abnormal result does not automatically mean you have a disease. Always discuss your results with a qualified healthcare provider.

Photosynthesis Explained: How Plants Make Their Own Food

Photosynthesis is the process plants use to turn sunlight into food. Without it, life on Earth would not exist – because photosynthesis produces the oxygen we breathe and the food we eat.

The Simple Version

Plants take in: Sunlight (energy), Water (through roots), Carbon dioxide (from air). They produce: Glucose (sugar for energy) and Oxygen (released into air).

The Chemical Equation

6CO₂ + 6H₂O + sunlight → C₆H₁₂O₆ + 6O₂

In words: Six molecules of carbon dioxide plus six molecules of water, using sunlight energy, produce one molecule of glucose and six molecules of oxygen.

Why Photosynthesis Matters

  • Oxygen production – Almost all oxygen in Earth's atmosphere comes from photosynthesis.
  • Food source – Every animal depends directly or indirectly on photosynthesis.
  • Carbon dioxide removal – Plants pull CO₂ from the atmosphere.
